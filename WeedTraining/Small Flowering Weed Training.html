Small-Flowering Weed Training

Mouse-ear Chickweed (Cerastium fontanum)

Mouse-ear chickweed grows on stiff, hairy stems that can reach up to 8 to 16 inches tall. The leaves are oval-shaped, and grow to be .4 to 1 inch long. The flowers grow in open clusters with 5 deeply divided white petals.






 

Corn Speedwell (Veronica arvensis)

 

Corn speedwell grows along the grown when young, but developing upright stems as it matures and flowers. The lowers leaves are hairy with rounded teeth on the edges, and grow on stalks. The upper leaves are narrow, hairy and grow directly off the stem.

 

Field Madder (Sherardia arvensis)

Field madder grows on hairy, square stems, and can grow either erect or along the ground. The flowers grow in clusters at the ends of stems and are pink to purple in color. The leaves are hairy, elliptic or lanceolate in outline, and taper to a distinct tip.

 

Field Pansy (Viola rafinesquii)

Field pansy plants can reach up to 12 inches in height. The leaves are oval shaped, but taper towards the base of the leaf. Field pansy flowers are white or blue, irregularly shaped and often have yellow markings.

 

Ground Ivy (Glechoma hederacea)

Ground ivy grows on square stems that are mostly hairless, and that grow in a creeping fashion along the ground. Ground ivy has round or kidney shaped leaves and the edges have rounded teeth. The leaves have a distinctive vein pattern that emanate from the same point at the base of the leaf. The “trumpet-shaped” flowers grow in clusters of 3 in the areas between the stems and the leaves, and they tend to be blue-violet and grow to be around .312 to .375 inches long.

 

Hairy Bittercress (Cardamine hirsuta)

Hairy bittercress grows on erect stems that branch in multiple directions from the plant base. The stems can grow as much as 12 inches tall. The leaves consist of clusters of 2 to 4 leaflets that grow along the central stem. Hairy bittercress flowers grow in clusters at the ends of flowering stems. The flowers are small, white and consist of 4 petals.

 

Henbit (Lamium amplexicaule)

 

Henbit grows on square stems with a green or purple tinge. Henbit leaves can reach up to 5 inches in length, and are circular to heart-shaped with rounded teeth on the edges. The flowers occur in whorls within the upper leaves and are pink or purple. It is easy to mistake henbit for purple deadnettle, but an identifying characteristic of purple deadnettle, the purple hue of upper leaves, does not occur in henbit.

 

Indian Mockstrawberry (Duchesnea indica)

 

Indian mockstrawberry grows in a creeping fashion along the ground. Each Indian mockstawberry leaf is composed of three leaflets that are oval-shaped to elliptical and can grow up to .75 to 3 inches long. The leaves are wide, hairy and have small rounded teeth along the edges. The yellow flowers grow alone at the end of long stalks, and consist of five petals. The berries are red, fleshy and bear a resemblance to real strawberries. Indian mockstrawberry plants resemble wild strawberry plants, however the main identifying characteristic is the yellow flowers – wild strawberry plants have white flowers.

 

Lyre-Leaved Sage (Salvia lyrata)

Lyre-leaved sage grows on an upright, square-shaped stem that can reach 1 to 2 feet in height. The leaves grow mostly at the base of the plant, and have 3 deep lobes that become tinged with purple in the winter. The flowers grow on the upper part of the stem, and are pale blue to violet color and trumpet-shaped.

 

Purple Deadnettle (Lamium purpureum)

 

Purple deadnettle has distinctive purple-red flowers that grow in groups of 3 to 6 in the upper leaves of the plant. The plant itself can grow to be 16 to 18 inches tall. The leaves have a circular outline with scalloped edges. The upper leaves of the plant tend to become purple, while the lower leaves remain green. The upper leaves are also more triangular than the round lower leaves. It is easy to mistake purple deadnettle for henbit, but an identifying characteristic of purple deadnettle, the purple hue of upper leaves, does not occur in henbit.

 

Yellow Woodsorrel (Oxalis stricta)

Yellow woodsorrel grows on pink to white stems that tend to grow closer to the ground, and can reach up to 20 inches tall/long. The leaves grow along the stems in clusters of 3 heart-shaped leaflets. The flowers grow in clusters at the ends of long stalks, and the individual flowers consist of 5 yellow petals.

 

Spreading Dayflower (Commelina diffusa)

Spreading dayflower grows along the ground and branches heavily. The leaves can be either lanceolate or oval shaped, and grow to around .5 to 2 inches long in North American varieties. The flowers are small and pale blue.

 

Spring Beauty(Claytonia virginica)

Spring beauty grows on a flowering stem that can reach 3 to 6 inches in height. The stems are light green to reddish green in color. The leaves are linear to lanceolate, with one distinct vein along the length of the leaf. The leaves can grow to be around 3 to 6 inches long and .25 inches wide. The flowers have 5 pink petals and are .25 inches wide when fully open.

 

Doveweed (Murdannia nudiflora)

Doveweed stems are not usually visible, but can sometimes be seen very close to the grown. The leaves are linear in outline and grow from .75 to 4 inches long with soft hears on the upper edges. Doveweed flowers grow in small clusters on the ends of short stalks. The flowers are blue to purple in color. The fruit grows small and green.

 

White Clover (Trifolium repens)

White clover grows on close to the ground on green stems. The leaves are composed of groups of 3 or less commonly 4 egg-shaped leaflets that can grow to be .5 to 1.25 inches long. White clover leaflets also have a distinctive white or lighter green “V Marking” close to their base. White clover flowers grow on stalk, with rounded flower heads that are around .5 to 1.25 inches long and consist of 20 to 40 individual white flowers. .
