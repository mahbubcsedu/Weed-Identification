Grasslike Weed Training

Annual Bluegrass (Poa annua)

       

You can recognize bluegrass by the tuft of cobwebby hairs at the base of each floret, by a parallel, translucent line on either side of the leaf’s midrib, and by the abruptly incurved leaf tip resembling a boat prow in three dimensional shape. Annual bluegrass is distinct from other species of bluegrasses by the leaf blades’ rugosity, referring to the crinkled or puckered area that runs across some blades about one-third the distance from the tip.

 

Annual Sedge (Cyperus compressus)

Annual sedge grows on a triangular stem and grows leaves in three vertical rows, with extensive branching. Annual sedge can grow to be from 4 to 18 inches tall. The leaves are linear and taper to a point. It has a distinctive flat seedhead with spikelets at the ends of the stem. It can be distinguishes from other sedges (purple or yellow nutsedge) by the fact that it is shorter and the leaf blades are narrower.

 

Baha'i Grass (Paspalum notatum)

Bahai grass is a low-growing, creeping grass. The leaves are flat, tough-textured and hairless that fold and inroll to a point.

 

Bermudagrass (Cynodon dactylon)

Bermudagrass has flat leaf blades, and grows upright in line with the stem. Leaf blades tend to reach a length of 7 cm. Bermudagrass typically flowers between May and October, and the flowers range in color from green to purple.

 

Large Crabgrass (Digitaria sanguinalis)

Large crabgrass grows along the ground, with many branching stems which are densely haired. The grass blades are 1.25 to 8 inches long. It is easy to confuse this weed with goosegrass, but the main distinguishing feature is that goosgwrass has a distinct white center and crabgrass does not.

 

Dallisgrass (Paspalum dilatatum)

Dallisgrass grows with erect stems that gain a reddish tint with increasing maturity. Dallisgrass can reach a height of 5 feet. Dallisgrass leaf blades grow from 4 to 12 inches in length. The seedpods grow on the tips of spikes.

 

Goosegrass (Eleusine indica)

Goosegrass grows on green stems that radiate outward from a distinctive white center. The leaf blades grow to be around 2 to 14 inches long, with a fold along the middle of the blade. Goosegrass seedheads grow in spikes at the tops of stems and can be found in clusters of 2 to 13 spikes that range from 1.5 to 6 inches long. It is easy to confuse this weed with crabgrass, but the main distinguishing feature is that groosgrass has a distinct white center and crabgrass does not.

Path Rush (Juncus tenuis)

Path rush grows on slender, hollow dark green stems that have small, rounded greenish-brown flowers at the tip.

 

Purple Nutsedge (Cyperus rotundus)

Purple nutsedge grows on erect, unbranched stems that are triangular. The leaves are dark green and have a distinctively shiny appearance and ridging along the middle vein. The leaves also taper abruptly to a sharp point. Purple nutsedge also has purple seedheads that grow in spike at the ends of solitary stems.

 

Wild Garlic (Allium vinaele)

Wild garlic flowers grow with flowering, slender stems that can reach up to 1 to 3 feet in height. The leaves are thin, round and hollow. Wild garlic flowers grow at the ends of the flowering stems, and are greenish-white. The flowers grow above the purple-red globe of ovoid bulblets

 

Yellow Nutsedge (Cyperus esculentus)

 

Yellow nutsedge grows on erect, triangular stems that produce spikelets on the ends. The leaves are yellow to green in color, have distinctive ridging along the middle vein and gradual taper to point. Yellow nutsedge grows yellow spikelets at the ends of stem.

 

Cheat Grass(Bromus tectorum)

Cheat grass grows rolled with hairy leaves and sheathes with drooping seedheads.

 

Broadleaf Plantain (Plantago major)

The broadleaf plantain has oval shaped leaves around 5 to 20 cm long and 4 to 9 cm broad. Each leaf has 5 to 9 noticeable veins. Its flowers are small, brown-green and have purple stamens.

 

Buckhorn Plantain (Plantago lanceolata)

Buckhorn plantain has narrow linear leaves with noticeable veins running along the length. Distinctive flowering spikes grow at the tips of stalks.
